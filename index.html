Guide: The Scoring Method & Practice Tips

The big idea

Will Robins started coaching in 2008 and noticed most students asked him to fix their swing so they could hit it farther — but what they actually wanted was to shoot lower scores. That gap became The Scoring Method: a course-management-first approach that tells you what club to hit, where to hit it, and exactly how to practice the parts of your game that are actually costing you strokes. The core philosophy is simple: keep the big numbers off the scorecard first, then worry about swing technique.

The five practice categories on your card

1 Putt Circle — short putts PP 1

Putts from a set distance where 1-putt should be the expectation. Set your own target distance (e.g. 3–5 ft) and target score (e.g. 8 putts for 8 balls = 100% one-putt), then log each attempt.

2 Putt Circle — long putts PP 2

Lag putts from farther out where the realistic goal is a stress-free 2-putt. Track how often you leave yourself a tough second putt, not just whether you made it.

3 Shot Circle — chipping PP B

Up-and-down practice inside roughly 25 yards of the green. Robins' own tracking advice: log the distance of your first putt after each chip — if you're regularly leaving 12–15 footers from inside 25 yards, that's the leak, not your putter.

3 Shot Scoring Zone — wedges PP B

Full-swing wedge shots (roughly 25–100 yards from the green) where the target is proximity, not just "on the green." Set a target yardage from the green you should be beating consistently.

Go To Club PP A

Your reliable off-the-tee or approach club: the one that keeps the ball in the fairway width you set, even if it isn't your longest option. The goal isn't distance — it's eliminating penalty strokes and blow-up holes.

Tracking a live round: Entering vs. Down in the Scoring Zone

Robins splits every hole into two halves: Entering the Scoring Zone — the shots it takes to get the ball inside 100 yards of the hole — and Getting Down in the Scoring Zone — everything from there until it's holed. Add the two together and that's your score for the hole. On a par 4, for example, the target is entering the zone in 2 shots, then getting down in 2 (one approach, one putt) for par. The Round tab in this app mirrors that: enter your Entering (A) and Down-in (B) shots per hole and it totals your score (C), front/back/round totals, and putts automatically.

Track proximity by zone, not just putts

One of Robins' clearest diagnostic tips: log the distance of your first putt on every hole for a full round (or several), then average it by which zone your approach shot came from. It tells you where your strokes are really going before you blame your putting.

Minimize blow-up holes first

Before refining any of the drills above, Robins recommends a simple course-management filter: keep the ball in play at all costs. Homework: play 18 holes (then build to 72) with zero penalty strokes, even if that means leaving driver in the bag. Fewer double-and-worse holes lowers your average score faster than any swing change.

Commit to every shot

Scoring is as much mental as mechanical. After each shot, rate your commitment from 1–10 — did you fully visualize the shot before you hit it, or were you still deciding over the ball? Low commitment scores are often a bigger cause of bad shots than swing faults.
